Linear algebra gives data science a compact language for representing
academic datasets. A vector can store one student's weekly study hours,
quiz score, or attendance count. A matrix can store the same variables
for an entire class, with rows representing students and columns
representing measured features.

The dot product measures alignment between two vectors. If one vector
contains study hours and another contains model weights, the dot product
combines them into a weighted score. Matrix multiplication applies this
idea to many students at once, making it possible to calculate
predictions for a whole cohort.

Projection is useful for regression because a fitted line can be
understood as the projection of observed outcomes onto the space spanned
by the input features. This connection explains why geometry and matrix
operations are prerequisites for understanding least squares.
